What is a RSI?

RSI (Relative Strength Index) is a technical momentum indicator that measures the magnitude and change of price movements. It was developed by J. Welles Wilder and first introduced in his 1978 book. The RSI oscillates between zero and 100 and is typically used to identify overbought or oversold conditions in a traded security.

RSI Calculation: RS (Relative Strength) = Average of 'n' day's up closes / Average of 'n' day's down closes. RSI = 100 - (100 / (1 + RS)) RSI Interpretation: Overbought and Oversold: When RSI reaches 70 or above, it might be considered overbought, indicating a potential downward reversal. If RSI drops to 30 or below, it might be considered oversold, suggesting a potential upward move. Divergences: If the asset’s price is rising but the RSI is dropping, it may signify a weakening of the trend and a possible reversal. Conversely, if the asset’s price is falling but RSI is rising, it might also signify weakening of the trend and a potential upward reversal. Centerline: When RSI crosses above the 50 level, it might be an indication of a potential bullish trend. When RSI crosses below the 50 level, it could be an indication of a bearish trend. It's important to understand that, like all technical indicators, RSI is not always accurate and is best used in conjunction with other indicators and methods of analysis.
